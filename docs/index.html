🧩 Why emoji break in WHMCS

An emoji is one or more Unicode code points, and every layer between your keyboard and your customer's screen has to agree on how to store and display them. In a WHMCS stack there are several layers that often disagree:

  • The database. Older installs use the utf8 MySQL charset, which despite the name only stores three bytes per character and cannot hold most emoji. The full set needs utf8mb4. Save a four-byte emoji into a three-byte column and it becomes ???? or is truncated on the spot.
  • Email. A template may render fine in the admin preview, then arrive in a mail client that substitutes a tofu box for a symbol it does not have a glyph for.
  • Age. Newer emoji from recent Unicode releases are simply unknown to older systems, which fall back to a placeholder.

The failure is quiet. The admin area looks fine, the customer sees a broken box, and nobody notices until a client mentions it. The safest emoji are the older, simpler, single-code-point symbols that every layer has understood for years, and that is what the copy board above is filtered to.

✅ What tends to work, and what does not

✅ Usually safer

  • Basic symbols and older Unicode emoji
  • Simple single-code-point emoji
  • Emoji without skin tone modifiers
  • Emoji without gender or family combinations

⚠️ Often unsupported

  • Country flags
  • New emoji from recent Unicode releases
  • Skin tone variations
  • Complex zero-width-joiner sequences

Even a safer emoji is worth a real test in your own stack before it goes into a billing-critical or support-critical message. The list here is a strong starting point, not a promise about your specific database, PHP version, and mail pipeline.

💬 Questions people ask

How does the search work?

Every emoji carries the official Unicode CLDR keywords (the same set that powers phone emoji keyboards) plus a curated layer of colors and concepts. So you can search by name (heart), by idea (love, danger, zodiac), or by color (red, green), and it still finds the right symbol if you make a typo like hart or chekc. It all runs in your browser, with no lookups sent anywhere.

How were these chosen?

They are drawn from the older, widely-supported Unicode ranges that predate the four-byte emoji explosion, the symbols that render consistently across old and new systems. The list is reviewed by hand rather than generated wholesale, which is why it is deliberately conservative.

My emoji still broke. What now?

The most common cause is a database still on utf8 instead of utf8mb4. Converting the relevant tables and the connection charset fixes the majority of cases. If it renders in the database but breaks in email, the mail client is the likely culprit, and a plainer symbol is the safe move.

Can I contribute an emoji that works, or one that breaks?

Yes, and real test reports are the most valuable thing here. Open an emoji report with the emoji, your WHMCS and PHP versions, your database charset, and where you tested it.

Is this only for WHMCS?

The compatibility rules apply to any older PHP and MySQL stack, so the list is useful well beyond WHMCS. The framing is WHMCS because that is where product names, email templates, and client-area labels make the breakage most visible to paying customers.
